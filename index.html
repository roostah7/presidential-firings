Methodology & caveats

Scope (applied consistently across all 27 terms from 1933 onward): Cabinet secretaries, Cabinet-rank officials (USTR, OMB / Bureau of the Budget Dir., EPA Admin., UN Ambassador, CIA Director, DNI, OSTP Dir. when Cabinet-rank, NRA Administrator under FDR, Special Assistant for Disarmament Affairs under Eisenhower), senior White House staff (Chief of Staff & Deputy CoS, NSA & Deputy NSA, Homeland Security Advisor, Press Secretary, Communications Director, WH Counsel, NEC Director, Special Counsel / Special Assistant to the President when filling top-advisor role, Chief Strategist), and FBI Director. Excludes sub-Cabinet agency heads (CDC, FEMA, CISA, IRS, USAID Deputy), service secretaries (Navy/Army/Air Force), US Attorneys, Inspectors General, Deputy Press Secretary, ambassadors other than UN, and uniformed military officers (e.g. MacArthur).

"Forced" means: publicly fired, asked to resign, demanded to step down, or pushed out under scandal/policy break. Voluntary departures (career moves, retirement, run for office, end-of-term, health, death) are excluded. Lateral moves and promotions within the same administration are not double-counted (e.g. Mulvaney moving OMB → acting CoS, Kissinger losing NSA hat but keeping State).

Term assignment: A departure is assigned to whichever term the firing/decision occurred in. Cisneros (Jan 1997) is in Clinton T1; Card (March 2006) is in Bush T2; Petraeus (Nov 2012) is in Obama T1; Jones (Jan/Feb 1945) is in FDR T3 since the decision was announced before Jan 20, 1945 even though Wallace was confirmed in March.

Pre-WWII caveats: The "senior White House staff" concept applies more loosely to FDR — the EOP wasn't created until 1939, the Chief of Staff role wasn't formalized until Eisenhower's Sherman Adams, and the National Security Advisor role was created in 1953. Pre-1953 counts are therefore weighted heavily toward Cabinet figures. The chart should be read knowing the "denominator" of senior positions has grown over time.

Borderline calls: Sebelius (Obama T2), Mattis and Cohn (Trump 1), Ickes (Truman T1), Durkin (Eisenhower T1), and Stassen (Eisenhower T2) are all counted as forced because they resigned in policy protest after losing the president's confidence. Musk (Trump 2) is counted because his exit was accelerated by a public break with Trump even though the 130-day SGE limit was a formal trigger. McFarlane (Reagan T2) is contested; included here as forced. Hardin (Nixon T1) excluded as voluntary per most sources.

Sources: Brookings Institution turnover trackers (modern presidents), Wikipedia "Cabinet of [President]" pages and "List of [President] administration dismissals and resignations," contemporaneous reporting from major outlets, biographical entries on individual officials, articles on specific events (Cabinet Massacre 1979, Saturday Night Massacre, Halloween Massacre, Iran-Contra, Watergate, Signalgate). Confidence is high for Truman onward; FDR-era data is somewhat thinner and "forced vs. voluntary" lines were less sharply drawn.

"Worst at hiring": Trump 1's 7.50/year is in a class by itself — about 5× the rate of the next non-Watergate term and ~30× Biden. Nixon T2 at 5.16/year is essentially Watergate concentrated into 18 months. Trump 2 is currently tracking at 3.88/year (still going). FDR T4 (~82 days) and Clinton T2 are the only terms with zero qualifying forced departures.
